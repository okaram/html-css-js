HISTORY.  Since July 5, 1990, Marietta First Christian Church (Disciples of Christ) has “spread the love of Christ” by providing emergency food to families in need.  The Disciples Mission Food Pantry has been featured in the Atlanta Journal-Constitution, the Marietta Daily Journal and on Atlanta TV Channels 2 (ABC), 5 (FOX), 11 (NBC) and 46 (CBS).  It is where families in the City of Marietta and Cobb County go who have nowhere else to turn for help.  The financial contributions and food that you give helps 500-750 families each month.  The Food Pantry was originally founded by the Christian Women’s Fellowship and led by Anita Farris and Marge Rose.  For the past fifteen years, its Executive Director has been Sherwood Smith, a Pastoral Associate at F.C.C.  Volunteers pick up food donations, shop for food, stock food, pack food bags, keep records, register and assist clients on Mondays and Saturdays from 9:00 am to noon.

 

DIRECTOR'S MESSAGE.  I recently read an article that was trying to indicate how hard the recession had hit the middle class.  I thought to myself, I really don’t know.  The families that the Disciples Mission Food Pantry assists are not in the middle class.  Most, I believe, have never made it to middle class status.  They are the unemployed and very poor, homeless and sometimes employed but usually very poor, the part-time working poor, the full-time working poor, and the middle class which for any number of reasons has fallen out of the middle class into the poor and very poor.  We are told that twenty percent of the people asking for help report that it’s the first time in their lives that they have ever asked for help.  At our Food Pantry, first time new families average between 15-20 percent in any one month.

 

Imagine working all your life, paying your bills, making charitable contributions, and even volunteering; and, then, you lose your job, your home is foreclosed upon, you lose your car, and, last but not least, your health care ends.  Now, your out knocking on doors looking for a job – any job – but you’re told that you’re over-qualified, under-qualified, too young, too old, not enough experience, haven’t worked recently enough, or any of a hundred other reasons not to be hired.  How is a person expected to maintain their sense of confidence, will-power, and dignity?

 

Currently, recipients of State funds are required to pay ($17) for their own drug testing within 48-hours if they are approved for TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) from the State Department of Family and Children Services.  And, of course, the State Legislature is looking at requiring food stamp recipients to earn their GED, pursue technical education, attend self-development classes, or enroll in adult literacy classes in order to receive their benefits.  It’s  wonderful, when the State helps people with opportunities, training and support for finding work, but placing all these stipulations on low income families attempting to feed their families and struggling to survive during the current crisis may be a too heavy a burden for many.  How we treat these families, the little children, and the “least of these” among us is one of the most important moral issues of our time – of all times.

 

And, it is the children that suffer the most.  According  to the latest statistics, one in four children are food insecure in America and one in five children go to bed hungry.  Children are the fastest growing segment of the population moving into poverty.  In addition, more than one in ten seniors are in poverty, one in three households headed by a single woman are food insecure, and over one in four black and Hispanic households are also food insecure.  But, the worst thing that is happening is that approximately 17 percent of our veterans are homeless, and, younger veterans (age 20-25) have it harder with up to 36 percent homeless.  And, overall, 40-43 percent of homeless men are veterans.

 

And what is food insecurity?  The Federal government defines it saying:  “that at times during the year, the food intake of household members was reduced and their normal eating patterns were disrupted because the household lacked money and other resources for food.”  However, the Oxford Dictionary has a more common sense definition:  “The uneasy or painful sensation caused by want of food.”

 


FOOD PANTRY STATISTICS.  During April, the Food Pantry served 569 families with 674 children, 153 seniors and 1,857 total family members with 22,605 pounds of food, and spent $295.12.  In May, the Pantry served 493 families with 519 children, 137 seniors and 1,490 total family members with 19,548 pounds of food, and spent $.955.20.

 

RESULTS OF MARCH – APRIL FUNDS DRIVE.  During March through April each year, we undertake our annual funds drive when a portion of all funds and food donated was matched by the Feinstein Foundation.  This year’s funds drive was not the usual success of previous years for several reasons.  First, due to the lack of funds at the end of last year, we were forced to seek a major infusion of funds over the holidays and into the beginning of this year.  Therefore, and because of the success of that funds drive, we didn’t place as much emphasis on our March – April funds drive.  Second, Palm Sunday and Easter fell in the middle of this period with competing competition for donations.  But we still raised over $4,000.  Praise God!

 

COBB FAITH PARTNERSHIP ANNUAL FORUM.  The Cobb Faith Partnership annual forum was held on May 1st, 2012.  This is an all day seminar where various faith-based and other agencies exhibit and information sessions are held to assist attendees with their individual missions to help the poor and needy families throughout Cobb County.  This year, I represented the Disciples Mission Food Pantry on three panels discussing homelessness and poverty.

 

THE POST OFFICE LETTER CARRIER’S ANNUAL FOOD DRIVE.  The Letter Carrier’s annual food drive was a qualified success this year, although we still received approximately 6,150 pounds of food.  Unfortunately, one of the major national sponsors of the food drive did not distribute the usual post cards advertising the food drive to Florida and Georgia.  Coupled with a lack of advertising generally, the food drive only raised a little over half of what has been raised in previous years.  However, we are still thankful for the more than three tons of food received and our thanks go out to the individual letter carriers who collected the food, and to the twenty-five volunteers who helped pick up the food at the Sandy Plains Post Office, sorted the food in the Fellowship Hall, and stored the food over at the house.

 

DONATIONS OF BOX-TOPS FOR EDUCATION.  This year, we were blessed to be able to make two large donations of “box-tops for education” to two elementary schools, Park Street E.S. and LaBelle E.S.  We thank all who participated by donating labels, and in cutting off the many thousands of labels, especially, Paddy Kern and her friend Mary.

DONATIONS OF CAMPBELL’S SOUP LABELS.  Again, this year, we were blessed to be able to donate to our regional church thousands of Campbell’s Soup labels.  Thanks to the many people who donated labels or who cut off labels from products donated to our food pantry, especially the soup donated during the annual Letter Carrier’s food drive in May.  Joe Kern was able to recently present a bag full of labels to the Georgia Regional Office of the Christian Church (Disciples of Christ).

 

FOOD PANTRY DEVOTIONAL WORSHIP SERVICE STARTED.  A particular Food Pantry success was the establishment of a short devotional worship service prior to the opening of the pantry on each Monday and Saturday.  Scripture is read, a devotion is presented, prayers are said, and the song “This is the Day that the Lord has made…” is sung.  All this lasts approximately ten minutes before the start of registration and enhances our service to the needy families who come to us for food.  Anyone who would like to participate or host this devotional worship service is invited.

 

A LOOK FROM THE OTHER SIDE.  One of our clients, Jasmine Harvey, writes:  “I’m married with three children and I am very grateful for the food pantry.  I’m a nursing assistant and my husband is a truck driver.  With his hours at his job diminishing and my job not paying all that much, money is scarce.  So coming to the pantry has been a life saver.  I thank everyone at the church for being so kind and for the prayers that seem to be getting us through this difficult time.”

 

And, Ashley Terrell, a P.E. teacher at Park Street Elementary School writes:  “I wanted to thank y’all so much for the amazing amount of Box Tops!  Thank you for choosing to make a difference for our children!  We look forward to your continued support.  Thank you, Thank you, THANK YOU!!”
